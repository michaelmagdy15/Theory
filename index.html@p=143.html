General Navigation

1. Earth, Solar System

THE EARTH

· Meridians

  • Lines joining the poles
  • Every meridian has an anti-meridian
  • They form lines of constant longitude
  • Prime Meridian = Greenwich (000º E/W)
  • They reach up to 180º E/W
· Parallels of latitude.

  • Have an east/west direction
  • Reference = Equator (00ºN/S)
  • They reach up to 90º N/S
  • Remarkable Parallels: Tropic of Cancer (23.5º N), Tropic of Capricorn (23.5ºS)

ORBIT OF THE EARTH

· Definitions regarding the orbit of the Earth:

  • Aphelion: Point of the orbit where the Earth is furthest from the Sun (Approximately 4th July)
  • Perihelion: Point of the orbit where the Earth is closer to the Sun (Approximately 4th January)

· Kepler Laws:

  • First Law. Planets describe elliptical orbits with the Sun located at one of the two foci.
  • Second Law. The line joining the Sun and a planet sweeps equal areas in equal times.
  • Third Law. The period of an orbit is proportional to the radius of the orbit (T² = kr³)

SIGNIFICANT PLANES

· Planes and significant definitions:

  • Plane of the Equinoctial: Plane of the Equator extended into space.
  • Plane of the Ecliptic: Plane of the orbit of the Earth around the Sun. Also described as the apparent trajectory of the Sun around the Earth. Forms an angle of 23'45º with the Equator.
  • Equinoxes: When the Sun crosses the plane of the Equinoctial
  • Solstices: When the Sun reaches its top and bottom latitudes (June and December, respectively)

GREAT AND SMALL CIRCLES

· Great Circle: Circle whose centre and radius are those of the Earth itself.

  • Shortest distance between 2 points.
  • Great Circles are always closer to the poles than rhumb lines.
  • Vertex: The most northerly and southerly point of a Great Circle. The direction of a Great Circle can only be 090º or 270º in those points.
  • Both vertex of a Great Circle are antipodal.

· Small Circle: Anything that is not a great circle

· Rhumb Lines: Lines that cross every meridian at the same angle.

2. Direction

INTRODUCTION

· Definitions

  • Bearing: Direction of a point measured to / from another point.
  • Heading. Direction in which the nose of an aircraft is pointing.
  • Reciprocal. Opposite direction (+-180º)
  • Relative Bearing. Bearing measured with reference to the nose of the aircraft. Bearing = Heading + Relative Bearing
  • Rhumb line. Line of constant direction
  • Track or course. The path of an aircraft over the Earth
  • Variation. Angle between True North and Magnetic North

ESTABLISHING OUR REFERENCES

· True North

  • It's the point where all meridians intercept. It's also the point where the axis of rotation of the Earth intercepts the surface (at its northerly position)
  • All angles measured against true north will be referred to as "true".

· Magnetic North:

  • It's a point on Earth's surface at which the magnetic field is perpendicular to the surface.
  • A compass will align itself with the horizontal component of the Earth's magnetic field, thus, pointing to the Magnetic North.
  • Magnetic North Pole is in constant movement at a rate of 15 km/year. An oscillation is completed every 960 years.
  • Because of this constant movement, magnetic variation at a fixed point constantly changes.
· Magnetic Variation

  • It's the difference between magnetic north and true north.
  • Variation east, magnetic least
  • Variation west, compass best

· Compass deviation

  • It's the difference between magnetic north and compass north, due to inaccuracy and interference with systems of the aircraft.
  • Deviation east, compass least
  • Deviation west, compass best

· Rule of thumb

· Remarkable lines:

  • Agonic lines join all points of the earth's surface having zero magnetic variation. They converge at magnetic and geographic poles.
  • Isoclinic lines join all points of equal magnetic dip.
  • Isogonal lines: Lines joining points of equal magnetic variation.

Note: Magnetic dip is the angle between Earth's surface and the magnetic field component. It's 0 at the Magnetic Equator and 90 at the Poles.

3. Distances

INTRODUCTION

· The Nautical Mile

  • A nautical mile is is the distance of a minute of latitude along any line of longitude. Defined by ICAO as 1852 metres
  • For simplification, we assume the Earth to be a perfect sphere when calculating distances. Therefore, we can assume that 1 nautical mile is also the distance of 1 minute along the Equator of the WGS-84 ellipsoid.

· In real life, the distance distance covered by 1 minute of arc changes because of the irregularity of the Earth

  • Shortest minute is at the Equator (6046 ft)
  • Longest minute occurs at the poles (6108 ft)
  • At approximately 48º, 1 minute corresponds to 6080 ft

DEPARTURE FORMULA

· Calculating Earth circumference length

  • Because the Equator is a whole circle, it covers 360º. Each degree has 60' of longitude. Therefore, there are 21600 minutes in total in the Equator
  • Since 1 minute equals 1 nautical mile, we can assume that the length of the Earth's circumference at the Equator is 21600 nautical miles
  • Note that the length of the Earth's circumference will decrease with increasing latitudes since meridians get closer

 

We can conclude that the distance between 2 points at the same latitude = Change in longitude (in minutes) * cos (latitude)

· For example, the distance between 45ºN 004ºE and 45ºN 020ºE is:

  • Change in longitude= 16º = 960'
  • Distance = 960' x cos (45º) = 678 nm

Note: Along a meridian, the distance between two points is the difference of latitude. Meridians have constant circumferences.

GREAT CIRCLES

· Great circles join two points at the surface of the Earth through the shortest route.

  • This is the typical "curved" trajectory of aircraft as seen in a map
  • However, following them can be complicated since it changes its heading constantly through the whole great circle.

· Great circles vortex

  • The northernmost and southernmost points of the Great Circle
  • They are located at each other's anti-meridian

RHUMB LINES

· Rhumb lines cross all meridians at the same angles

  • Easy and comfortable to fly since heading is always the same
  • However, not the most economical

DISTANCE CONVERSIONS

Typical units:

  • 1 nm = 1852 metres
  • 1 nm = 6080 feet
  • 3'28 ft = 1 m

AIR MILES

· Ground vs air miles

  • A nautical ground mile is one nautical mile over the ground.
  • A nautical air mile is one nautical mile through the air mass.

· Effect of wind on Ground and Air miles

  • Headwind increases nautical air miles, for a given ground distance.
  • Tailwind decreases nautical air miles, for a given ground distance.

4. Form of the Earth

INTRODUCTION

· Three ways of representing the shape of the Earth.

  • Sphere
    • Simplest way to represent Earth. However, a bit inaccurate
  • Ellipsoid
    • A bit more accurate and still relatively simple way to represent Earth.
    • This is the model used by ICAO World Geodetic System 84
  • Geoid
    • A complex way to represent Earth, since the result is an irregular shape.
    • This is the most accurate model and is obtained through the measurement of the values of gravity at different points of the Earth.

FORM OF THE EARTH

· Compressibility of the Earth

  • Earth is flattened at the poles. This is called compressibility or flattening.
  • Equatorial Radius = 3444 nm
  • Polar Radius = 3433 nm
  • The polar diameter is 1/300 less than the equatorial diameter.
  • Earth Compressibility = (SemiMajor Axis - SemiMinor Axis) / SemiMajor Axis

REPRESENTATION OF LATITUDES

· 2 main ways of representing latitude:

  • Geocentric latitude uses the centre of the Earth as the datum.
  • Geodetic latitude is measured by drawing a line perpendicular to the surface of the Earth.
  • Maximum difference between both occurs at 45º N/S, resulting in 11.6º disagreement.
  • A difference of zero exists at 0º and 90º latitude

5. Earth Convergency

EARTH CONVERGENCY

· Earth Convergency is the change of heading that an aircraft flying a Great Circle experiments.

  • It's zero for a route flying along the Equator
  • Maximum at the poles

· Earth Convergency = Change of longitude x Sine of Mean latitude

CONVERSION ANGLE

· The difference of heading between a Rhumb Line joining 2 points and a Great Circle is called "Conversion Angle". It's half the Earth Convergency.

· Also note that, halfway through the Great Circle route, track is the same as the Rhumb Line track

6. Chart

INTRODUCTION

· The ideal chart should have:

  • Correct directions and angles
  • Scale would be equal over the entire chart
  • Great circles as straight lines
  • Rhumb lines also as straight lines

· This is impossible and we have to prioritize some.

PROPERTIES

· Conformality

  • Conformality: A chart is conformal if meridians and parallels cross at right angles, scale is roughly the same in all directions and angles and shapes are not distortioned.
  • Projections which preserve areas are called equivalent or equal-area projections. They either preserve areas everywhere, or distort them everywhere. Not used for navigation because they can not be conformal.
  • All charts used in navigation are conformal.

· Scale variation

  • It's a measure of the distortion applied to the chart

· Scale factor

  • Scale factor = Chart distance / Reduced Earth Distance

· Ortomorphism

  • Implies conformality and, in addition, meridians and parallels must cross at right angles, even if parallels are curved

SCALES

  • Scale = Chart Distance / Earth Distance
  • Using scales, we can represent big areas in small surfaces and viceversa

DEVELOPABLE SURFACES

· Developable surfaces are those surfaces that you can turn into a flat layer without deforming them. A sphere, for example, isn't developable (try peeling an orange and flatten it, it's impossible without deforming it)

  • Flat sheet: Used for Polar Stereographic charts
  • Cone: Used in Lambert Conformal charts
  • Cylinder: Used in Mercator charts

8. 1 in 60 rule

INTRODUCTION

· 1 in 60 rule is used in practical navigation to simplify calculations

· It states that, for 60 miles traveled, being 1 mile off track indicated a Track Error of 1 degree.

· Imagine the following scenario:

  • We have traveled a leg of 60 miles
  • We happen to be 5 nm left of the intended track
  • Well, using the 1 in 60 rule, we can assume that our track error is 5º!
  • This way, we can easily correct the drift and make calculations to resume our planned path

· 2nd example: We have traveled 30 miles and we happen to be 2 nm left of track

  • If in 30 miles we have been blown 2 miles to the left, if we don't take any corrective action, in 60 miles we will be 4 nm left of track
  • Therefore, we can assume track error to be 4º
  • Same applies for different distances

· Some people find it easier to work with the formula:

  • Angle = (Distance off / Distance gone) * 60

CALCULATIONS DERIVED FROM THE 1 IN 60 RULE

· Height on a glideslope = DME * 100 * GS Angle

· Required Rate of Descent on glideslope = (GS * 100 * GS Angle) / 60

· Runway slopes (degrees) = (Difference in threshold elevations / Runway length) * 60

· Runway gradients (percentage) = (Difference in threshold elevations / Runway length) * 60

· Angle of track = (Distance off track / DME Range) * 60

9. Time

· Declination: the angle of the Sun with respect to the Equator.

  • The length of day/night at a given latitude varies with the declination.
  • The rate of change of declination, therefore, is equivalent to the rate of change of the length of daylight (usually max rate at Equinoxes)
  • Changes of seasons are governed by the declination.

DEFINITIONS OF DAY

· A day may be defined as the time taken for the Earth to rotate once about it's spin axis.

  • Measurements against the Sun are called "Solar"
  • Measurements against a star are called "Sidereal"

· Civil day

  • Should be based on the Sun
  • Should be of constant length

· Apparent Solar Day.

  • Measured against real Sun.
  • Issue: Not of constant length (days are longer in summer)

· Mean Solar Day

  • Average length of the apparent solar day
  • Of constant length, related to light and darkness
  • Basis of LMT (Local Mean Time)
  • Maximum difference between mean time and real time occurs in November (16 min) and in February (14 min)

· Sidereal Day:

  • Measured against distant stars, so the effect of the Earth traveling around the Sun is negligible
  • Nearly of constant length
  • Not related to light and dark, so it's not suitable for daily use

DEFINITIONS OF TIME

· Local Mean Time (LMT)

  • When the Sun crosses a given meridian, the Local Mean Time at that meridian is 1200 hrs
  • When the Sun crosses an anti-meridian, the Local Mean Time at the reference meridian is 2400

· Greenwich Mean Time (GMT)

  • It's the LMT along the Meridian of Greenwich.

· Universal Coordinated Time (UTC)

  • Used in aviation.
  • In practice, similar to GMT. Theoretical difference is that UTC is measured by using atomic clocks, while GMT still references the Sun.
  • It's the same for all parts of the Earth

TIME ZONES

· Arc of time:

  • 24 hours = 360º
  • Therefore, 1 hour = 15º of longitude

· Calculating LMT

  • In order to calculate LMT at a given point, we have to account for 1 hour each 15º of longitude change.
  • When moving east, local time is ahead of UTC
  • When moving west, local time is behind UTC

· Summertime / Daylight saving time

  • Some countries alter their local time during summer in order to reduce electricity consumption and cost saving

SUNRISE AND SUNSET

· Sunrise and sunset definitions

  • Sunrise: The moment when the upper rim of the Sun appears on the horizon in the morning
  • Sunset: The moment when the Sun is no longer visible on the horizon at the evening

· Twilight definitions

  • Civil twilight: The centre of the Sun is between the horizon and 6º below it
  • Nautical twilight: The centre of the Sun is between 6º and 12º below the horizon
  • Astronomical twilight: The centre of the Sun is between 12º and 18º below the horizon

10. CRP Conversions

· SAT vs TAT.

  • SAT (Static Air Temperature) is the actual temperature of the air.
  • TAT (Total Air Temperature) is the temperature of the air affected by the friction as the aircraft travels through the air. It's always greater than SAT (as long as movement exists)

· Specific Gravity = Mass of fuel / Mass of water

  • Weight (kg) = Specific Gravity x Volume (liters)
