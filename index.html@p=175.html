Mass and balance

1. Introduction

TERMINOLOGY

Allowed Take-off MassThe mass taking into consideration all possible limitations for take-off including Regulated Take-off mass and Regulated Landing Mass
Area Load or Flood LoadThe load distributed over a defined area. Units of measurement are typically kg/m²
Basic Empty MassMass is the mass of an aeroplane plus standard items such as: unusable fuel or fluids; lubricating oil; fire extinguishers; pyrotechnics; emergency oxygen equipment
Dry Operating MassMass of the aeroplane ready for a specific type of operation, excluding usable fuel and traffic load, but including crew and baggage, catering and removable passenger service equipment and portable water and lavatory chemicals.
Maximum Zero Fuel Mass (MZFM)Maximum weight allowed without accounting for the fuel on-board
Maximum Structural Landing Mass (MSLM)The maximum permissible total mass of the aircraft on landing
Maximum Structural Take-off Mass (MSTOM)The maximum permissible total mass of the aircraft at commencement of take-off
Operating MassThe Dry Operating Mass plus fuel
Performance Limited Landing MassThe mass subject to the destination airfield limitations. It can't exceed structural limits.
Performance Limited Take-off MassThe take-off mass subject to departure airfield limitations. It must never exceed structural limits.
Regulated Landing MassThe lower of Performance Limited Landing Mass and Maximum Structural Landing Mass
Regulated Take-off MassThe lower of Performance Limited Take-off Mass and Maximum Structural Take-off Mass
Running LoadThe load distributed over a defined length of a cargo compartment. Units of measurement are typically kg/m or lb/ft
Traffic LoadThe total mass of passengers, baggage and cargo, including any non-revenue load (this is generally what makes money)
Variable LoadWeight of the crew, of items such as the crew's baggage, removable units, and other equipment the carriage of which depends upon the role for which the operator intends to use the aircraft for the particular flight
Useful LoadTraffic load + Take-off fuel. Called useful because an aircraft without fuel or passengers/cargo is useless

· Passenger classification for mass and balance purposes

  • 12 years and above: adults
  • 2-11 years: children
  • Less than 2 years: infants

2. Operational requirements

· Basic Empty Mass: Mass of an airplane plus standard items such as:

  • Unusable fuel
  • Other unusable fluids
  • Lubricating oil in engine and auxiliary units
  • Fire extinguishers
  • Pyrotechnics
  • Emergency oxygen equipment
  • Supplementary electronic equipment

EU-OPS 1.605 - MASS AND BALANCE (GENERAL)

· Operators must establish the mass and the CG of any aeroplane by actual weighing prior to initial entry into service, and, thereafter, at intervals of:

  • 4 years if individual aeroplane masses are used
  • 9 years if fleet masses are used
  • The accumulated effects of modifications and repairs on the mass and balance must be accounted for and properly documented.

· An operator must determine the mass of all operating items and crew members by weighing or by using standard masses.

· An operator must establish the mass of traffic load, by actual weighing or in accordance with standard passage and baggage masses specified in OPS 1.620

EU-OPS 1.605 - MASS AND BALANCE (MASS VALUES FOR PASSENGERS AND BAGGAGE)

· Operators shall compute the mass and balance of passengers and checked baggage by using the actual weighed mass of each person and baggage, or by using standard values, unless the number of passenger seats available is less than 10, in which case those masses may be established verbally.

APPENDIX 1 TO OPS 1.605

· Determination of the DOM of an aeroplane

  • Weighing of an aeroplane
    • New airplanes are typically weighed at the factory and placed into operation without reweighing unless there's a significant alteration to the airplane.
    • Individual mass and CG position of each airplane shall be re-established periodically, never exceeding 4 years for individual masses and 9 years for fleet masses.
    • The CG position must be established by weighing or calculation.
  • Fleet mass and CG position
    • For a fleet of aeroplanes, an average DOM and CG position may be used provided that the masses of each individual aircraft are within certain tolerances:
      • If the mass of an aeroplane varies by more than 0'5 % of MSLM or the CG position varies by more than 0'5 % of the MAC from the fleet CG, the aeroplane must be omitted from that fleet.
    • Interval between 2 fleet mass evaluations must not exceed 48 months

· Configuration of the airplane for BEM measurement

  • Airplane must be fully equipped for the intended role, including:
    • Unusable fuel
    • Unusable oil (or full oil if MTOM < 5700 kg)
    • All other operating fluids fully serviced
    • Extra devices that are always there (fire extinguishers, etc)

· Weighing procedure

  • Weighing must be accomplished by manufacturer or by an approved maintenance organization
  • Weighing must be done in an enclosed building with air conditioning off
  • Airplane must have at least 3 points of support

3. Centre of Gravity

EFFECT OF EXCEEDING MAXIMUM MASS

· Effect on lift

  • Increasing the mass (weight) means that the aircraft has to produce more lift. Generally, it can do so by either increasing airspeed or angle of attack, since density of the air is out of our control and wing surface area is fixed.
  • Increasing airspeed means that parasite drag will increase, and thrust required to hold that airspeed will increase
  • Increasing angle of attack means that induced drag will increase, and, therefore, thrust required to hold a given airspeed will increase

· Effect on performance

  • Performance is reduced (takeoff and landing distances are now longer, angle and rate of climb are reduced, maximum speed is reduced too)
  • Stalling an landing speed is increased
  • Maneuverability is reduced
  • Wear on tyres and brakes is increased
  • Structural safety margins are reduced

EFFECT OF EXCEEDING CG POSITION LIMITS

· Exceeding forward CG limit

  • Elevator must generate a lot of downforce to compensate the moment generated (which depends on the distance between CG and CP)
  • This downforce acts like "added weight", and the aircraft will need to generate more lift. Direct consequences from this are:
    • Increased stall speed
    • Increased angle of attack, which involves increased induced (and total) drag
    • Reduced range and endurance
    • Increased fuel consumption
  • A forward CG involves positive longitudinal stability. However, if we exceed the forward limit, stability will be so high that pilots will experience a lack of elevator authority.
    • Stick forces required are high

· Exceeding afterward CG limit

  • Longitudinal stability reduced
    • For any pitch change, stick forces required are low
    • Flight controls become excessively effective
    • Risk of overstressing the aircraft
  • Spins tend to get flatter, difficulting recovery
  • However, range and endurance increases, and fuel consumption will decrease because now the elevator doesn't need to generate downforce.

· In conclusion, it's more economical to fly with a CG close to the rearward limit, but obviously not exceeding it for safety reasons.

MOMENTS

· Definitions

  • Centre of Gravity Datum. This is a fixed point on the aircraft from which all measurements are taken in the calculation of the CG.
  • Arm. Distance between the datum and a mass
  • Moment. It's the turning effect of a mass around the datum. Mathematically, it's expressed as Moment = Arm * Weight
    • By convention, clockwise moments are positive, and anti-clockwise moments are negative

CALCULATION OF THE CENTRE OF GRAVITY

The calculation of the centre of gravity, in general, follows these steps:

  • 1) Calculate the moments of every mass loaded into the aircraft
  • 2) Calculate the total weight
  • 3) Divide total moment / total weight and you will get a resulting total arm. Well, that's your CG position!

4. Last minute changes to CG

· Some exercises involve accounting for a last minute change on the CG by either adding, subtracting or moving mass.

· If you don't want to memorize any formulas, keep in mind that the methodology is the same as we've previously seen. All you need to do is apply (or remove) the new moments from the take-off or landing moments of the airplane.

  • Remember that, if we are moving weight from the front of the aircraft to the back, we have to REMOVE the weight at the front and insert it at the back. It's a typical mistake.
